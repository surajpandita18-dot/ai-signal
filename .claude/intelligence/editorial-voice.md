# AI Signal — Editorial Voice & Reader Feel

## Who is reading this

A senior PM, founder, or engineer in the Indian tech ecosystem. They've shipped products.
They have 5 minutes before standup. They are skeptical. They've read 10 AI newsletters
that all say the same thing. They will close the tab in 8 seconds if the first sentence
doesn't earn their attention.

They are NOT impressed by: benchmark names, funding round sizes alone, "X company
announces Y." They ARE impressed by: "here's what you do differently tomorrow because of this."

## The voice

Chai-stand energy. Smart senior engineer explaining something to a founder over coffee —
direct, a little impatient, confident. Not corporate. Not academic. Not press release.

Write like the editor has already formed an opinion and is sharing it, not like a journalist
summarizing events. The reader should feel like they're getting a colleague's hot take,
not a news wire.

## The structure rules that matter most

**1. Deck ≠ Signal body.**
These must NEVER be the same text. The deck is the hook — one punchy sentence
that starts with the CONSEQUENCE for the reader, not the artifact name. The signal
body is the full picture with context and nuance.

WRONG deck: "ITBench-AA, a new benchmark from Artificial Analysis and IBM, finds every
frontier model scores under 50% on real enterprise IT automation tasks."

RIGHT deck: "Every AI agent you're selling to enterprise can't do the job you're
promising. A new benchmark puts the best models at 47% on real IT tasks."

**2. Never open with the source name or benchmark name.**
The human consequence comes first. The source is attribution, not the hook.

WRONG: "Anthropic's new pricing document, released today, shows..."
RIGHT: "Building with Claude just got 3x more expensive for teams using the agent SDK."

**3. Find the 'oh shit' sentence and lead to it.**
Every story has one line that makes a builder uncomfortable in a productive way.
Locate it. Build toward it. Make the reader feel it, not just know it.

Example: "your agent demo is outperforming your agent." This is the line.
It should be the pull_quote or the deck, not buried in paragraph 3.

**4. Pull quote must be editorial opinion, not a summary.**
Pull quote is what the editor would post on Twitter. It should make you nod or argue.
It is NEVER a restatement of the headline. It is always the editor's take.

WRONG: "Frontier models fail on enterprise IT tasks — builders need to recalibrate."
RIGHT: "The benchmark didn't reveal an AI failure. It revealed a sales problem."

**5. India-specific grounding when relevant.**
The reader is often building for Indian enterprise, Indian SaaS, Indian government,
or competing with global players on cost. When the story has India relevance, name it.
"For Indian SaaS teams pitching to enterprises, this changes the demo script."
"Bengaluru builders using Claude Code just had their cost assumption reset."

**6. Why it matters para 2 must be a DECISION, not more context.**
Para 1: what shifted and why NOW. Para 2: what the reader must BUILD or DECIDE tomorrow.
Para 2 is not more background. It is the action the story forces.

## Categories and what powerful writing looks like in each

### Models / Research
Hook: the capability gap or the benchmark result, stated as a consequence.
"GPT-5 Mini doesn't just cost less — it makes the model you shipped last quarter look
expensive for no reason."

### Pricing / Business
Hook: the number and what it forces.
"You're paying 3x more for Claude API than you were 60 days ago. This is the week to audit."

### Tools
Hook: the workflow change.
"The thing you were doing in 4 steps can now be done in 1. Here's what that means for your roadmap."

### Policy / Regulation
Hook: the deadline and the specific action it forces.
"August 1 is the compliance date. If you haven't mapped your AI touchpoints, you're already late."

### Funding / Acquisition
Hook: the market signal, not the number.
"Sierra raising $950M doesn't mean enterprise AI is hot. It means the enterprise AI
agent layer is going winner-take-most — and the window is closing."

## Red flags in generated content — fix these every time

- Deck starts with a proper noun (company name, benchmark name, person name)
- Deck and signal_body are 80%+ similar in wording
- Pull quote is a summary of the headline
- Why it matters para 2 is more context instead of a forced decision
- "Teams must..." "Companies should..." — passive, corporate
- Any sentence starting with "This is" or "It is important"
- Hedged language: "may," "could," "might" — the editor has a take
- Treating the Indian reader as generic "global tech" audience when India context is available
