# Interview Prep Brief — Quality Rubric

The bar for AI, Basically.'s Job Signal interview question is **prep that lands an offer at Anthropic / OpenAI / a hire-bar AI org**. Not "generic PM trivia". Each brief must be the *single best free resource on the internet* for its specific question.

References (voice + structure): Aakash Gupta (Product Growth — long-form deep), Paul Huynh / Lenny's contributors (tighter punch), with AI, Basically.'s calm-confidence voice.

---

## The 8 sections every prep brief must have

| # | Section | Word count | Purpose |
|---|---------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | The question | 30-80 | Verbatim, scenario-grounded (real production setup, not abstract case) |
| 2 | Why they ask this — what they're really testing | 100-150 | The judgment dimension. The thing that separates hire from no-hire. Why the obvious answer is the trap |
| 3 | The framework (named) | 150-250 | A named, ordered 4-6 step approach. Each step gets 1-2 sentences. The framework must be *reusable* across similar questions |
| 4 | Sample answer (60-90 sec spoken, annotated) | 300-450 | First-person, written as actually spoken. Sidebar `[why this works]` annotations on key moves (3-5 annotations per answer) |
| 5 | How deep to go on first pass | 80-120 | What to lead with vs. hold for the probe. The 60-second version vs. the 3-minute version |
| 6 | Counter-questions you'll face | 300-500 | 3-5 likely follow-ups. For each: strong answer + `[why this works]` + common weak answer + why it loses |
| 7 | The traps that downlevel candidates | 150-250 | 3-5 specific wrong moves with why each loses signal |
| 8 | Good vs great — what separates hire from strong-hire | 80-120 | The one move that takes the answer from "competent" to "interviewer wants to hire them" |
| ✦ | Meta-skill — what this transfers to | 60-100 | One sentence: the durable skill this question tests, that applies to ANY question of this class |

**Total target: 1250-2050 words per brief.** Long enough to be the definitive resource. Short enough that a candidate reads + internalizes it in 8-10 minutes the night before the interview.

---

## Quality bar — 7 axes (1-5 each, must score ≥4 on every axis)

### 1. Specificity / production-realism
**Score 5:** scenario is one a real AI PM at Anthropic / OpenAI / a regulated lender / a foundation model lab has actually faced. Names a real metric, real constraint, real time horizon. Bad: "Imagine you're launching an AI product..." Good: "Six months into shipping AI underwriting at an Indian lender, the RBI asks: how do you know your model isn't worse than humans for SC/ST applicants?"

### 2. Framework reusability
**Score 5:** the named framework transfers to 3+ adjacent question classes. Not a one-shot trick. Bad: "Use the AARRR funnel." Good: "Counterfactual → slice → observability → kill criteria — same shape for any 'is this AI safe enough to ship' question."

### 3. Sample-answer realism
**Score 5:** reads as a candidate actually talking, not a written essay. Includes hesitation moves ("the way I'd think about it…"), concession moves ("that depends on…"), and concrete numbers. Annotated callouts explain *why* a move signals senior judgment.

### 4. Counter-question depth
**Score 5:** counters probe the 3-4 dimensions of the framework. Not generic "tell me more". Each counter has both the strong answer AND the weak answer with explicit "why this loses".

### 5. Trap precision
**Score 5:** traps are specific wrong moves (not generic "don't be vague"). Each trap names the move + names the seniority signal that gets lost. Example: "Naming a kill threshold post-hoc instead of pre-committed — signals you treat measurement as theater, not commitment."

### 6. Good-vs-great distinction
**Score 5:** the strong-hire signal is one specific move, not a list of platitudes. Example: "Hire candidates name the comparator. Strong-hire candidates name the comparator AND propose how they'd communicate a fail to the board pre-launch." A reader should be able to identify which one they currently are.

### 7. Voice
**Score 5:** calm-confidence. No FOMO ("you NEED to know this"), no false intimacy ("hey, just between us"), no LinkedIn cadence ("Here's the thing nobody tells you..."). One smart honest friend explaining things. Indian context where natural; no forced Hinglish in product copy.

## Hard rules

1. **Real scenario** — every question is anchored in a production AI situation. Names a real stack, regulator, market, or constraint.
2. **Named framework** — every brief carries a memorable framework name the reader can repeat ("Counterfactual → slice → observability → kill criteria").
3. **First-person sample answer** — written as spoken, ≥3 annotated `[why this works]` callouts.
4. **3+ counter-questions with full handling** — each shows strong answer + weak answer + why weak loses.
5. **3+ traps** — each names a specific move and the seniority signal lost.
6. **Meta-skill** — one durable transferable skill, not "be specific" platitude.
7. **No filler** — every paragraph earns its place. If a paragraph can be cut without losing prep value, cut it.

---

## Anti-patterns (auto-fail the rubric)

- ❌ "It depends" as an answer — every brief must commit to a specific recommended move
- ❌ Star method scaffolding (Situation/Task/Action/Result) — too generic, not AI-specific
- ❌ Generic PM advice repackaged ("focus on the user") — must be specifically about the AI judgment dimension
- ❌ Sample answer in third person ("a strong candidate would say…") — must be first-person, spoken
- ❌ Counter-questions without strong+weak answers — both sides are mandatory
- ❌ Traps stated as principles ("avoid being vague") — must be specific wrong moves
- ❌ More than 2050 words — discipline, not bloat
- ❌ Less than 1250 words — depth, not summary

---

## Test for the rubric

After writing or reviewing a brief, ask:

1. If a PM had a real Anthropic / OpenAI screen on Thursday, would they read this Wednesday night? (If no → not deep enough)
2. Would the candidate using this brief give a noticeably *better* answer than one who just thought about the question alone? (If marginal → framework isn't doing work)
3. Is there one specific move in the sample answer that would make an interviewer write "STRONG HIRE — pattern match to senior" in their notes? (If you can't point to that move → strong-hire signal missing)
4. Does the meta-skill apply to 3+ other questions the candidate might face? (If no → too narrow)

A brief is **DONE** when:
- All 7 quality axes score ≥4
- Word count is in the 1250-2050 range
- All 7 hard rules are met
- No anti-patterns triggered
- All 4 tests above answered "yes"

A brief is **NULL-PASS** (no improvement found) when:
- A reviewer with the rubric in hand cannot identify a specific concrete change that would raise any axis score

**Polish loop stop signal**: 5 consecutive null-pass iterations across all 3 briefs in rotation.
